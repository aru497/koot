Cost of Living in Regional Australia for Students vs Sydney & Melbourne

K Koott · 9 min read · updated June 2026

The cost of living in regional Australia for students is one of the few numbers that can quietly decide whether your whole plan works. Tuition and flights are fixed and obvious. Rent, groceries and transport are not — and over a two-year degree, the difference between a sharehouse in Sydney and a sharehouse in Newcastle or Adelaide can add up to a serious sum, often the equivalent of several lakhs of rupees.

This guide breaks down where your money actually goes, how regional cities compare to Sydney and Melbourne, and how the Department of Home Affairs' A$29,710 living-cost benchmark fits into the picture. We've kept every figure as an honest range — exact rents change month to month and suburb to suburb, so we'll always point you to where you can check the current numbers rather than inventing a precise one.

What "regional" actually means in Australia

"Regional" sounds like it should mean a tiny town in the middle of nowhere. For student-visa and migration purposes, it doesn't. Australia classifies a long list of substantial cities as regional — places with universities, airports, hospitals, beaches and Malayali grocery stores. The big distinction is simple:

  • Not regional: Sydney, Melbourne and Brisbane — the three largest, most expensive cities.
  • Regional: Adelaide, Newcastle, Wollongong, Hobart, Darwin, Townsville and many others — large enough to live comfortably, but with lower costs and a post-study visa advantage.

That single classification is why the cost-of-living conversation matters so much. Choosing a regional city isn't a compromise on city life; for most Indian students it's a way to get a similar experience for meaningfully less money. The three costs that actually move your budget

It's easy to get lost in spreadsheets of small expenses. In practice, three categories decide whether your year is comfortable or stressful: rent, food and transport. Everything else — phone plans, the occasional movie, a Saturday cricket match — is rounding error by comparison.

1. Rent — the single biggest line

Accommodation is the largest cost for almost every student, and it's also where regional cities save you the most. A shared room or a room in a sharehouse is dramatically cheaper than a studio, and regional sharehouses are cheaper again than their Sydney or Melbourne equivalents. The cheapest setups are usually:

  • A room in a shared house with other students (often the best value);
  • University-managed or purpose-built student accommodation (convenient, sometimes pricier);
  • Homestay with a local family, useful in your first months while you find your feet.

Because rent moves the most, it's also the figure most worth researching for your specific city. The Australian Bureau of Statistics (abs.gov.au) publishes rent and price data, and the official Study Australia site keeps living-cost guidance current.

2. Food — cook and the gap is huge

Groceries in Australia are reasonable if you cook, and brutal if you don't. A student who shops at Coles, Woolworths or Aldi and cooks rice, dal and vegetables at home will spend a fraction of what someone eating out four nights a week spends. Indian and Kerala staples — rice, lentils, spices, coconut — are widely available, and regional cities almost always have at least one Indian or Asian grocer. The real variable here isn't the city; it's your habits.

3. Transport — and the regional advantage

This is where geography quietly helps. Many regional cities are compact enough to walk or cycle, and concession public-transport fares are common for students. In the biggest cities, longer commutes and pricier fares add up. Some regional areas even offer transport concessions to international students that Sydney and Melbourne historically have not — another small saving that compounds over a degree.

The headline is consistent: groceries are broadly similar across Australia, but rent and transport are where regional living wins, and rent is the line that dominates a student budget. Save 20–30% on your largest cost and the whole year changes shape.

The cheapest city is the one where your biggest expense — rent — is lowest. For most students, that points away from Sydney and Melbourne.

The A$29,710 visa figure, explained

When you apply for a student visa, the Department of Home Affairs expects you to show you can support yourself. For a single student, the living-cost benchmark is currently about A$29,710 for the year, on top of your tuition fees and travel costs. This is a real figure from the Department of Home Affairs — and it's a benchmark for your visa application, not a budget cap you have to spend.

Important

Verify before you apply. Visa settings and the living-cost figure change over time. Always confirm the current amount and the exact requirements on the official Department of Home Affairs website at immi.homeaffairs.gov.au before lodging anything. Treat any figure you read online — including this one — as a starting point to check, not gospel.

Here's the part agents rarely explain to Kerala families: A$29,710 is the same benchmark whether you study in central Melbourne or in Adelaide. But your real spending in a regional city can sit comfortably below that benchmark, which means the buffer works in your favour instead of against you. How regional living stretches a student budget

Put the pieces together and the regional advantage isn't a single discount — it's several smaller ones stacking up:

  • Lower rent on your biggest expense frees up money every single week.
  • Lower or no transport cost if you can walk or cycle to campus.
  • Lower tuition at many regional universities (more on that below).
  • The post-study visa bonus — graduates of a regional campus may be eligible for an additional 1–2 years on a Temporary Graduate (485) visa, depending on the area, which means more time to earn back your investment.

For a family in Kerala weighing the total cost of an Australian degree against an education loan, those factors compound. Lower outgoings during study plus more potential earning years after graduation is a very different equation from "expensive city, two years, then leave." Working while you study — and where the jobs are

Part-time work helps your budget breathe, and the rules are the same everywhere in Australia. Student-visa holders can work a capped number of hours during study — commonly summarised as 48 hours per fortnight while your course is in session, with more flexibility during scheduled breaks. As with everything visa-related, confirm the current cap, because it has changed before.

Check the current cap

For up-to-date work-hour rules and your rights as an employee in Australia, see the Fair Work Ombudsman and the Department of Home Affairs. Never assume a number from a forum is current.

Regional areas can be an advantage here too. Australia's Skills Priority List, published by Jobs and Skills Australia, tracks occupations in shortage — and many shortages are sharper in regional areas. Fields with genuine demand include Registered Nurses, Aged & Disability Carers, ICT and Software roles, Cyber Security, Civil and Mechanical Engineers, Early Childhood and Secondary Teachers, Social Workers, construction trades and agriculture roles. We go deeper in our guide to jobs in regional Australia. We won't quote you salary figures here — pay varies widely and we'd rather you check official sources than trust an invented number.

Frequently asked questions

What is the cost of living in regional Australia for students compared to Sydney?

Regional Australia is generally cheaper than Sydney and Melbourne, mainly because rent — the biggest line item in any student budget — costs noticeably less in cities like Newcastle, Wollongong, Adelaide, Hobart, Townsville and Darwin. Food, transport and entertainment also tend to be lower or comparable. The exact gap depends on the city and whether you share accommodation, so treat all figures as ranges and check current listings before you commit.

How much money do I need to show for an Australian student visa?

For a single student, the Department of Home Affairs uses a living-cost benchmark of about A$29,710 per year, in addition to your tuition fees and travel costs. This is the amount you should be prepared to demonstrate access to. Visa settings change, so always confirm the current figure on the official Department of Home Affairs website before you apply.

How many hours can a student work in Australia?

Student-visa holders can work a capped number of hours while their course is in session — commonly summarised as 48 hours per fortnight, with no cap during scheduled study breaks. The cap can change, so verify the current limit with the Department of Home Affairs and Fair Work before relying on a particular number.

Is studying in a regional area worth it for the lower cost of living?

For many Indian and Kerala students, yes. Lower rent stretches the A$29,710 budget further, regional universities often charge lower tuition, and graduates of a regional campus may be eligible for an additional 1–2 years on a Temporary Graduate (485) visa depending on the area. Combined, those factors can meaningfully change the maths of studying in Australia.

The bottom line

Groceries cost about the same wherever you study, but rent and transport are cheaper in regional cities — and rent is the line that dominates a student budget. Pair that with often-lower tuition and a possible 485 visa bonus, and the cost of living in regional Australia for students becomes one of the strongest reasons to look beyond Sydney and Melbourne. Just verify every visa and cost figure at the official source before you commit.
