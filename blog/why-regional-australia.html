Why Regional

Why Kerala & Indian Students Are Choosing to Study in Regional Australia

Koott9 min readupdated June 2026

For decades the default plan was simple: if you got into an Australian university, you headed for Sydney or Melbourne. That script is changing. More and more Kerala and Indian students are choosing to study in regional Australia instead — and not as a consolation prize. They are doing the maths on fees, rent, admission requirements and, crucially, the extra post-study work years a regional campus can unlock. This guide makes the honest case for regional study, including the trade-offs nobody puts in a brochure.

What "regional Australia" actually means for a student

This is the part most families get wrong, so it is worth nailing down first. "Regional" in the student-visa sense is a migration classification, not a description of how rural a place feels. The Department of Home Affairs designates certain areas as regional for student and graduate visa purposes — and the list is more generous than you would guess.

Cities that are designated regional for migration purposes include Adelaide, Newcastle, Wollongong, Hobart, Darwin and Townsville. These are real cities with beaches, universities, hospitals and airports — Newcastle and Wollongong are roughly an hour or two from Sydney. What is not regional: Sydney, Melbourne and Brisbane. So you can live in a genuine Australian city, study at a well-ranked university, and still sit inside the regional category that drives the visa benefits below.

Because these designations are set by government policy and can be updated, always confirm a specific campus's current status on the official Department of Home Affairs site before you commit. Our visa tools page also helps you sanity-check where a campus sits.

The cost case: where regional quietly wins

For most Indian families, money is the conversation that happens after everyone leaves the room. Here regional study has a real, structural advantage on two fronts.

Lower living costs

The second saving is rent and daily life. Housing, transport and eating out generally cost less outside Sydney and Melbourne, which is exactly where most international students cluster and bid up prices. The same part-time wage stretches further in Hobart or Townsville than it does in inner-city Sydney. We dig into the numbers in our cost of living comparison.

The number you must plan for

Wherever you study, your visa application has to show you can cover living costs. The Department of Home Affairs benchmark is currently about A$29,710 for a single student, on top of tuition and travel. Regional living does not change the benchmark you must show — but it usually means you actually spend less of it. Check the current figure and rules on the Department of Home Affairs site, and explore scholarships on our funding page.

Easier admission, without lowering your standards

Regional universities are typically more open in their entry requirements — many accept a minimum IELTS of 6.0 and reasonable, transparent academic cut-offs. For a student from Kerala with strong but not stratospheric marks, that can be the difference between an offer and a polite rejection from an oversubscribed Sydney program.

Important: easier admission is not lower value. Every university in the table above is CRICOS-registered, which means it is approved to teach international students and awards a nationally recognised Australian qualification. You are getting the same kind of degree — often from an institution that ranks respectably in the global rankings — with a clearer path in. The 485 visa bonus: the real reason students switch

This is the lever that turns a "maybe" into a "yes" for a lot of families. The Temporary Graduate visa (subclass 485) lets eligible graduates stay and work in Australia after they finish studying. For a Bachelor or a Master by coursework, the standard post-study work period is typically a couple of years.

Here is the regional twist: graduates of a regional campus may be eligible for an additional 1–2 years on a second or extended Temporary Graduate visa, depending on the regional-area category. That is one to two extra years of full-time Australian work experience, local income, and runway to build a migration case — earned simply by choosing where you study.

Two graduates, same degree, same year. One studied in central Melbourne; one studied in Adelaide. The Adelaide graduate can be eligible for extra post-study work time purely because of the regional classification.

One honest caveat, and it matters: visa rules and durations change. Day-counts, eligibility conditions and the regional categories are updated by the government from time to time. Treat every duration here as "up to an extra 1–2 years depending on the area," and verify the current settings yourself on the Department of Home Affairs site before you make a decision. Jobs and migration pathways are stronger where there's shortage

Extra visa years only help if there is work to do — and this is the second place regional quietly wins. Australia publishes a Skills Priority List through Jobs and Skills Australia identifying occupations in shortage, and many of those shortages are sharper in regional areas, where employers struggle to attract staff.

Real fields currently in demand on the list include:

  • Healthcare — Registered Nurses, and Aged & Disability Carers
  • Technology — ICT and Software roles, and Cyber Security
  • Engineering — Civil and Mechanical Engineers
  • Education — Early Childhood and Secondary Teachers
  • Community & professional services — Social Workers, and Accountants in some areas
  • Trades & primary industry — Construction trades and Agriculture roles

Choosing a course that maps to a shortage occupation does two things at once: it improves your odds of finding graduate work during those 485 years, and it can strengthen a future skilled-migration application. You can browse current demand on the Jobs and Skills Australia data portal, and read our deeper take in jobs in regional Australia. We avoid quoting salary figures here on purpose — pay varies widely, and you should rely on official sources rather than a number in a blog post.

While you study, you can also work part-time within the legal cap — commonly summarised as 48 hours per fortnight when your course is in session, usually with the cap lifted during scheduled breaks. The cap has changed before, so confirm the current limit on Fair Work and the Department of Home Affairs sites.

Real community, not just cheaper rent

Numbers aside, there is a human reason students stay regional: the experience is often warmer. Smaller cohorts mean lecturers learn your name. Housing is easier to find. And the Malayali and wider Indian diaspora is well established in cities like Adelaide, Newcastle and Hobart — there is usually a temple or church, a grocery that stocks the right spices, and a WhatsApp group that will help you find a room before you land.

The honest trade-offs

A guide that only sells you the upside is an advertisement, not advice. Regional study is the right call for many students — but not all. Weigh these honestly:

  • Fewer big-corporate offices on your doorstep. If your dream is a graduate role at a multinational headquartered in Sydney or Melbourne, those head offices are mostly in the non-regional cities. You may need to relocate after your studies — which can affect timing around your visa.
  • Smaller, quieter cities. Townsville or Armidale will not feel like Bengaluru or even Sydney. For some students the calm is the whole point; for others it takes adjustment. Be honest with yourself about the lifestyle you want.
  • Thinner part-time job markets in the smallest towns. Big regional cities like Adelaide and Newcastle have plenty of casual work, but the smallest centres have fewer hospitality and retail shifts competing for your hours.
  • Policy can change. The regional visa advantage exists because of current government settings. It has been generous for years, but it is policy, not a guarantee — which is exactly why every visa point in this article links you to the official source to verify.

None of these are dealbreakers for most students. They are simply the things an honest advisor would tell you across the table — so you choose with your eyes open.

The bottom line

If you want a recognised Australian degree, a lower total bill, a realistic shot at admission, and the best post-study runway to stay and work — regional Australia is, for a large share of Indian and Kerala students, simply the smarter starting point. Just verify the live visa settings before you commit, and pick a course that maps to real demand.

Frequently asked questions

What counts as regional Australia for student visa purposes?

Cities like Adelaide, Newcastle, Wollongong, Hobart, Darwin and Townsville are designated regional for migration purposes, while Sydney, Melbourne and Brisbane are not. The regional category can affect your eligibility for an extended Temporary Graduate (485) visa, so always confirm the current designation of a campus on immi.homeaffairs.gov.au before you apply.

Is it cheaper to study in regional Australia?

Generally yes. Regional universities often have lower published tuition than the big-city Group of Eight, and rent and living costs tend to be lower outside Sydney and Melbourne. You must still show the Department of Home Affairs living-cost benchmark of about A$29,710 for a single student, plus tuition and travel, when you apply for your visa.

Does studying regionally really add years to the 485 visa?

Graduates of a regional campus may be eligible for an additional 1 to 2 years on a second or extended Temporary Graduate (485) visa, depending on the regional-area category. Durations and eligibility change over time, so verify the current settings on immi.homeaffairs.gov.au rather than relying on any fixed figure.

Can I work while studying in regional Australia?

Student visa holders can work a capped number of hours while their course is in session, commonly summarised as 48 hours per fortnight, with the cap usually lifted during scheduled breaks. The cap changes from time to time, so confirm the current limit on the Department of Home Affairs and Fair Work websites.

Will a regional degree hurt my job prospects back in India or globally?

No. Regional universities are CRICOS-registered Australian institutions awarding the same nationally recognised qualifications as city universities, and several rank well in the QS World University Rankings. Many also sit in fields on Australia's Skills Priority List, which can strengthen both your local job prospects and your migration pathway.
