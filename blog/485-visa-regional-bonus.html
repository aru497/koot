The 485 Visa Regional Bonus: Extra Post-Study Work Years Explained

Koott 9 min read Updated June 2026

If you are weighing up where to study in Australia, the 485 visa regional bonus is one of the most practical reasons to look beyond Sydney and Melbourne. Graduating from a regional campus can hand you an extra one to two years of post-study work rights on top of the standard Temporary Graduate (subclass 485) visa — more time to build a career, earn, and strengthen a permanent residency case. For many Kerala and Indian students, those extra years are the difference between a short visit and a real future in Australia.

This guide breaks down what the 485 visa is, how the regional extension works, which areas actually count as "regional" for migration, the eligibility basics, and why this matters so much if you are chasing skilled work experience and a PR pathway. We keep the numbers honest: where exact rules and durations shift over time, we say so and point you to the official source rather than guessing.

What is the Temporary Graduate (subclass 485) visa?

The Temporary Graduate visa, subclass 485, lets eligible international graduates stay in Australia and work after they finish a recognised course. It is the bridge between studying on a student visa and either building a long-term career or moving onto a skilled or employer-sponsored visa. For a Bachelor's degree or a Master's by coursework, the standard post-study work period is typically a couple of years — enough to find a graduate role, gain Australian work experience, and start ticking boxes that migration pathways reward.

Crucially, the 485 is generally an open work visa: you are not tied to a single employer or even to the field you studied. You can take a graduate job, work casually, switch roles, and use the time to figure out your next step. That flexibility is part of why Australia remains a top destination for Indian students — and why the length of your 485 matters so much.

Because settings are reviewed and updated by government, you should always confirm the current eligibility rules and durations on the official Department of Home Affairs website before making decisions.

The 485 visa regional bonus, explained

Here is the headline. The standard 485 gives you a set base period of work rights. But if you study and live at a regional campus, you may become eligible for an additional one to two years on top — usually granted as a second or extended Temporary Graduate visa once your first one is nearing its end.

The exact length of the extension depends on the regional-area category where you studied. Australia groups regional areas into tiers, and graduates of the more remote categories can generally access a longer extension than those in larger regional cities. In plain terms:

  • Major cities (Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane) — standard 485 period, no regional extension.
  • Regional centres and larger regional cities (for example Adelaide, the Gold Coast, Newcastle, Wollongong, Hobart, Canberra) — eligible for an extension, commonly around one extra year.
  • Smaller cities and regional towns (more remote areas) — eligible for a longer extension, commonly up to two extra years.
Verify before you rely on it

Visa durations and area categories change. We deliberately describe the bonus as "up to an extra 1–2 years depending on the area" rather than quoting exact day counts. Always confirm the current second/extended Temporary Graduate settings on the Department of Home Affairs site for your specific campus and qualification before you enrol.

To qualify for the extension, you generally need to have lived, studied and met the regional requirements during your initial qualification, and to hold a valid first 485 visa. It is not automatic — you apply for the second visa — but for thousands of graduates it is one of the most valuable, lowest-effort advantages of choosing regional study in the first place.

Which areas count as "regional" for the 485 visa?

This is the part that confuses many families back home, because "regional" in the migration sense does not mean "tiny town in the middle of nowhere." For student-visa migration purposes, Australia treats everywhere except Sydney, Melbourne and Brisbane as regional. That single fact reshapes the whole decision.

So the following are all designated regional for student-visa migration purposes:

  • Adelaide (South Australia) — a capital city of well over a million people, classified regional.
  • Newcastle and Wollongong (New South Wales) — large coastal cities within easy reach of Sydney.
  • Hobart (Tasmania), Darwin (Northern Territory) and Canberra (ACT).
  • Townsville and other Queensland centres outside Brisbane, such as the Gold Coast and Sunshine Coast.

And these are not regional: Sydney, Melbourne and Brisbane. Study in those three and the regional 485 extension is off the table, no matter how good the university is.

Adelaide has a metro population north of a million — and it still counts as regional. That is the quirk smart students plan around.

Because the official area lists are reviewed periodically, treat the bullet points above as a guide, not a guarantee. Before committing, cross-check the postcode of your exact campus against current Home Affairs guidance and the CRICOS register, which confirms whether a provider and course are approved for international students.

Eligibility basics for the 485 and the extension

The 485 has a set of core requirements that apply to everyone, and the regional extension layers a few extra conditions on top. While you should always check the current detail on Home Affairs, the broad shape looks like this:

For the standard 485 visa

  • You completed a CRICOS-registered qualification that meets the Australian study requirement.
  • You hold or recently held an eligible student visa.
  • You meet age, English-language, health insurance and character/health requirements.
  • You apply within the required window after completing your course.

For the regional extension (second / extended 485)

  • You held a valid first Temporary Graduate visa.
  • You studied at and lived in a designated regional area while completing your qualification.
  • You continued to meet the regional residence conditions during the relevant period.

None of this is exotic, but the details — exact study periods, area categories, and the application window — are precisely the things that get tweaked from year to year. Why the regional bonus matters for PR

For many Indian students, the real prize is not just the degree — it is a credible path to permanent residency. Here the extra 485 years do quiet but powerful work in three ways.

1. More time to gain skilled work experience. Australia's skilled migration points system rewards Australian work experience in your nominated occupation. An extra one to two years on a 485 can be exactly what you need to accumulate enough qualifying experience to be competitive.

2. Access to regional migration points and pathways. Living and working in a regional area can attract additional points and open nomination pathways that simply are not available to big-city graduates. Combine that with the longer visa and the regional decision compounds in your favour.

3. Breathing room to get the timing right. PR applications are about hitting thresholds — English scores, experience, age, nomination. More legal work time means fewer rushed, expensive mistakes and more chances to improve your profile before you apply.

The compounding effect

Lower fees, easier entry scores, extra 485 years, and regional migration points — choosing a regional campus is one of the few decisions that improves nearly every part of the equation at once. It is why regional study has become a deliberate strategy, not a fallback.

Where the regional jobs actually are

Extra visa years only help if there is work to do. Encouragingly, many of Australia's most in-demand occupations have stronger demand in regional areas, which is exactly where your 485 wants you to be. Australia publishes a Skills Priority List through Jobs and Skills Australia that flags occupations in shortage.

Fields commonly identified as in shortage — many acutely so outside the big cities — include:

  • Health and care: Registered Nurses, Aged & Disability Carers, Social Workers
  • Technology: ICT and Software professionals, Cyber Security specialists
  • Engineering: Civil and Mechanical Engineers
  • Education: Early Childhood and Secondary Teachers
  • Trades, construction and agriculture roles
  • Accountants in some areas

We deliberately quote no salary figures here — pay varies widely by role, employer and location, and inventing numbers would not help you. To see live shortage data for a specific occupation, check the official Jobs and Skills Australia data, and read our companion guide on jobs in regional Australia for how to line up your course with where the work is.

Costs, work rights and the money you must show

Two financial facts are worth knowing up front, both straight from government sources.

The living-cost benchmark. When applying for a student visa, you generally need to show evidence of funds to support yourself. The Department of Home Affairs benchmark for a single student is about A$29,710 for living costs over a year, on top of your tuition and travel. Regional living is often cheaper than Sydney or Melbourne, which is one more reason the regional route stretches your budget further — see our regional cost of living guide for the breakdown.

Work hours while you study. On a student visa you can work a capped number of hours during your course — commonly summarised as 48 hours per fortnight — with generally unrestricted hours during scheduled course breaks. This cap has changed before and may change again, so confirm the current limit with Home Affairs and check your pay and workplace rights with the Fair Work Ombudsman. Frequently asked questions

What is the 485 visa regional bonus?

The 485 visa regional bonus refers to the extra post-study work time available to graduates of a regional Australian campus. On top of the standard Temporary Graduate (subclass 485) visa, eligible graduates of a regional university may qualify for an additional 1–2 years of work rights through a second or extended Temporary Graduate visa, depending on the regional-area category. Durations and rules change, so always verify current settings on the Department of Home Affairs website.

Which cities count as regional for the 485 visa?

For student-visa migration purposes, cities such as Adelaide, Newcastle, Wollongong, Hobart, Darwin and Townsville are designated regional. Sydney, Melbourne and Brisbane are NOT regional. Because classifications can change, confirm whether a campus address is regional using the official Department of Home Affairs guidance before you enrol.

Does the University of Adelaide count as regional?

Adelaide is classified as a regional area for student-visa migration purposes, so its universities — including the University of Adelaide, the University of South Australia and Flinders University — are generally treated as regional campuses. Always confirm the current designation on the Department of Home Affairs website, as area lists are reviewed periodically.

Do I need a regional university to get a 485 visa?

No. The standard Temporary Graduate (485) visa is available to eligible graduates of any CRICOS-registered course, including those in Sydney and Melbourne. Studying at a regional campus is what may unlock the extra 1–2 years on top of the standard period, plus access to certain regional migration points and pathways.

How many hours can I work on a student visa while studying?

Student-visa holders can work a capped number of hours during study — commonly summarised as 48 hours per fortnight — with generally unrestricted hours during scheduled course breaks. This cap changes from time to time, so verify the current limit with the Department of Home Affairs and check your pay and conditions on the Fair Work Ombudsman website.
