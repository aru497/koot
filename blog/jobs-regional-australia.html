Jobs in Regional Australia for International Students

K Koott · 9 min read · updated June 2026

If you are weighing up where to study Down Under, here is something most agents skip over: jobs in regional Australia for international students are often easier to find, sit in genuine shortage occupations, and tie directly into a stronger migration pathway. For students coming from Kerala and across India, that combination — real work while you study, plus a clearer route to staying on afterwards — is exactly what makes a regional campus worth a serious look.

This guide explains which occupations are actually in shortage (using Australia's own Skills Priority List), why regional areas struggle to fill them, the part-time work rules you must follow on a student visa, and how regional study and work feeds into your career and your 485 visa. Everything here points back to official government sources so you can verify the current settings yourself — because visa and work rules do change.

Why regional Australia has shortages

Australia's big three cities — Sydney, Melbourne and Brisbane — are not classified as regional for student-visa migration purposes. Most international students cluster there, which means the casual job market is crowded and competition for entry-level roles is fierce. Regional centres are the opposite story.

Places like Newcastle, Wollongong, Adelaide, Hobart, Darwin and Townsville are designated regional for migration purposes, and many of them have long-standing labour shortages. The reasons are structural rather than temporary:

  • Smaller local workforce. Fewer people live in regional towns, so there are simply fewer hands to fill rosters in hospitality, retail, aged care and farming.
  • Ageing populations. Many regional areas skew older, which drives steady demand for aged-care and disability-support workers and for healthcare more broadly.
  • Distance from big-city talent pools. Qualified professionals — nurses, engineers, teachers, accountants — often gravitate to the capitals, leaving regional employers short-staffed.
  • Seasonal industries. Agriculture, food processing and tourism create recurring spikes in demand that local labour cannot always meet.

For an international student, a shortage is an opportunity. It means employers are more willing to train, more flexible about hours that fit your timetable, and more likely to keep you on once you graduate. The Australian Bureau of Statistics (abs.gov.au) and Jobs and Skills Australia both track these regional gaps in their data.

In-demand occupations on the Skills Priority List

You do not have to guess which jobs Australia needs. Jobs and Skills Australia publishes the Skills Priority List (SPL), a national assessment of which occupations are in shortage and where. It is the single most useful document for any student thinking about career and migration, and it is free to read at jobsandskills.gov.au/data.

Across recent assessments, occupations that appear in shortage — many with stronger demand in regional areas — include:

  • Registered Nurses and other healthcare roles
  • Aged & Disability Carers and support workers
  • ICT and Software professionals, including developers and analysts
  • Cyber Security specialists
  • Civil and Mechanical Engineers
  • Accountants (in some areas and specialisations)
  • Early Childhood and Secondary Teachers
  • Social Workers
  • Construction trades — electricians, carpenters, plumbers and more
  • Agriculture roles, from farm management to food technology

Several of these map neatly onto degrees that Indian students already favour: nursing, IT and cyber security, engineering, accounting and teaching. If you are choosing a course, aligning it with a shortage occupation is one of the smartest moves you can make. We deliberately quote no salary figures here — pay varies widely by role, employer and location, and you should rely on current official data rather than numbers floating around online.

Check the list before you commit

The Skills Priority List is updated regularly and shortages shift year to year. Before locking in a course, read the latest SPL on jobsandskills.gov.au and cross-check the occupation against the official skilled occupation lists on immi.homeaffairs.gov.au. Part-time work rules: the 48-hour cap

Working while you study is not just allowed — it is part of how most students manage living costs and build local experience. But there are firm rules, and breaching them can put your visa at risk.

How many hours you can work

While your course is in session, student-visa holders can work a capped number of hours, commonly summarised as 48 hours per fortnight. A "fortnight" is any two-week period, so you cannot simply average it out across a month. During scheduled course breaks — semester holidays, for example — you can usually work unlimited hours.

This cap has changed more than once in recent years, so treat the 48-hour figure as a guide and confirm the current rule before you rely on it. The two authorities to check are the Department of Home Affairs (immi.homeaffairs.gov.au) for visa conditions and Fair Work (fairwork.gov.au) for your rights as an employee.

Your rights at work

One of the best things about working in Australia is that international students are protected by exactly the same workplace laws as everyone else. That means a national minimum wage, penalty rates for weekends and public holidays where awards apply, and protection from underpayment regardless of your visa status. Know your rights before you start — Fair Work publishes plain-English guides and a pay calculator.

  • You are entitled to at least the national minimum wage or the relevant award rate.
  • Cash-in-hand arrangements that dodge tax and superannuation are a red flag — they often hide underpayment.
  • You can report underpayment to Fair Work without affecting your visa.
A part-time job in a regional town is rarely just pocket money. It is your first Australian reference, your first local network, and often the foot in the door that turns into a graduate role. — The Koott view

How regional study and work helps migration

This is where regional study quietly outperforms the big cities. There are two distinct advantages working in your favour.

1. The regional 485 visa bonus

The Temporary Graduate visa (subclass 485) lets eligible graduates stay and work in Australia after finishing their degree. For a Bachelor or a Master by coursework, standard post-study work is typically a couple of years. The regional advantage is the extra time: graduates of a regional campus may be eligible for an additional one to two years on a second or extended Temporary Graduate visa, depending on the regional-area category.

That extra time is not a technicality — it is more runway to find skilled work, gain experience and build a case for permanent residency. Durations and eligibility rules change, so we keep this qualitative on purpose. Always confirm current settings at immi.homeaffairs.gov.au. We break the bonus down further in our guide to the 485 visa regional bonus.

2. Working in a shortage occupation

Skilled-migration pathways reward occupations Australia actually needs. If your degree and your work experience sit in a Skills Priority List shortage area — nursing, ICT, engineering, teaching, aged care — you are building exactly the profile that later skilled-visa pathways look for. Regional employment can also carry its own incentives within certain state and regional migration programs.

Put the two together: a regional degree (more post-study work time) plus experience in a shortage occupation (a stronger skilled profile) is a deliberately constructed migration strategy, not luck. Use our Visa Tools to map your situation, and check funding options on our Funding page.

A note on the financial requirement

When you apply for a student visa, you must show you can cover living costs. The Department of Home Affairs benchmark is about A$29,710 for a single student for a year, on top of tuition and travel. Part-time work helps with day-to-day expenses, but it is not meant to be your primary source of funds — plan your finances around this figure. Verify the current amount at immi.homeaffairs.gov.au.

How to start your job search

You will not need a job from day one, but lining one up early reduces stress and stretches your budget. A practical sequence:

  1. Get your paperwork ready. You will need a Tax File Number (TFN) and an Australian bank account before you can be paid properly. Set these up soon after arrival.
  2. Start with entry-level casual roles. Hospitality, retail, aged-care support and supermarket work are common first jobs and are often in steady demand regionally.
  3. Use your campus. University career hubs, job boards and student associations frequently list part-time and casual roles aimed at students.
  4. Tap the community. The Malayali and wider Indian community in regional towns is often the fastest route to a first job lead. Our Communities page helps you connect before you even land.
  5. Aim for relevant experience. Once settled, look for casual or internship work in your field of study — it is far more valuable for your career and migration than another shift behind a till.

For the wider list of things to sort out when you arrive — SIM, bank, transport, accommodation — see our Arrival guide.

Frequently asked questions

How many hours can international students work in Australia?

While your course is in session, student-visa holders can work a capped number of hours — commonly summarised as 48 hours per fortnight. During scheduled course breaks you can usually work unlimited hours. The cap can change, so always verify the current rule with the Department of Home Affairs and Fair Work before relying on it.

Which jobs are in shortage in regional Australia?

Australia's Skills Priority List, published by Jobs and Skills Australia, flags occupations in shortage. Fields with strong regional demand include Registered Nurses, Aged and Disability Carers, ICT and Software roles, Cyber Security, Civil and Mechanical Engineers, Accountants in some areas, Early Childhood and Secondary Teachers, Social Workers, construction trades and agriculture roles. Always check the latest Skills Priority List for current shortages.

Does studying in regional Australia help with migration?

Yes. Graduates of a regional campus may be eligible for an additional one to two years on a second or extended Temporary Graduate (subclass 485) visa, depending on the regional-area category. Working in an occupation that is in shortage can also strengthen a later skilled-migration application. Visa rules change, so confirm current settings at immi.homeaffairs.gov.au.

Are part-time jobs easier to find in regional areas than in big cities?

Regional towns often have persistent labour shortages and a smaller pool of available workers, so casual and part-time roles in hospitality, retail, aged care and agriculture can be easier to land than in saturated big-city markets. Pay and conditions are protected by the same national minimum wage and award system regardless of location.
